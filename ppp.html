On April 2, 2020 the Small Business Administration released final details regarding the Paycheck Protection Program application process. The release, titled the interim final rule can be found below.

The Paycheck Protection Program (“PPP”) authorizes up to $349 billion in forgivable loans to small businesses to pay their employees during the COVID-19 crisis. All loan terms will be the same for everyone.

Who can apply? All businesses – including nonprofits, veterans organizations, Tribal business concerns, sole proprietorships, self-employed individuals, and independent contractors – with 500 or fewer employees can apply. Businesses in certain industries can have more than 500 employees if they meet applicable SBA employee-based size standards for those industries.

For this program, the SBA’s affiliation standards are waived for small businesses (1) in the hotel and food services industries; or (2) that are franchises in the SBA’s Franchise Directory; or (3) that receive financial assistance from small business investment companies licensed by the SBA. Additional guidance may be released as appropriate.

What qualifies as included “payroll costs?” (Excerpts from page 10)

  • Payroll costs consist of compensation to employees in the form of:
  • Salary, wages, commissions, or similar compensation.
  • Cash tips or the equivalent (based on employer records of past tips or, in the absence of such records, a reasonable, good-faith employer estimate of such tips).
  • Payment for vacation, parental, family, medical, or sick leave.
  • Allowance for separation or dismissal.
  • Payment for the provision of employee benefits consisting of group health care coverage, including insurance premiums.
  • Employee Retirement expense.
  • Payment of state and local taxes assessed on compensation of employees.

What is expressly excluded from the definition of payroll costs? (Excerpts from pages 10-11)

  • Any compensation of an employee whose principal place of residence is outside of the United States
  • The compensation of an individual employee in excess of an annual salary of $100,000, prorated as necessary.
  • • Federal employment taxes imposed or withheld between February 15, 2020 and June 30, 2020, including the employee’s and employer’s share of FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) and Railroad Retirement Act taxes, and income taxes required to be withheld from employees. (It is interpreted that all Federal taxes, including FICA, are excluded)
  • Qualified sick and family leave wages for which a credit is allowed under sections 7001 and 7003 of the Families First Coronavirus Response Act.

Recommended Documentation

  • Application – linked below (note: # of employees is FTE’s)
  • Quarterly IRS 941’s for 2019
  • Detailed individual employee payroll report from on or around 2/15/2020
  • Invoices for health insurance premiums paid for by employer
  • Invoices for retirement plan administration
  • Documentation of state and local taxes assessed on compensation of employees

Loan Details

  1. Loan Amount = 2.5x the average monthly payroll expense from 2019 (less foreign employees or any compensation to individual employees over $100,000)
  2. Payment Deferral for 6 months
  3. After 8 weeks the borrower can apply for loan forgiveness
  4. If a portion, or the entire loan is not forgiven it would carry a loan rate of 1% and become due in 24 months form the initial loan closing
  5. Amount = 2.5x the average monthly payroll expense (to include 3-5 for above for total annual expense then divide by 12, please make sure to subtract wages over $100,000 on any employee that makes over that amount. If employee makes $120,000, then only $100,000 should be the annual wage)

Loan Forgiveness

  • 8 weeks following loan date the business owner must send documentation certifying the number of FTE’s, plus documentation verifying that a minimum of 75% of the loan funds were used for payroll.
  • Any shortfall may be supplemented by the inclusion of expenses related to mortgage interest, lease/rent payments &/or utility expenses.
